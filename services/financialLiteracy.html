Financial Literacy

There is widespread agreement that levels of financial literacy worldwide are low, especially in rural communities of Sub Saharan Africa, and that consumers tend to overestimate their financial skills and knowledge. The lack of basic financial literacy skills such as budgeting, saving, banking and managing money has weighed heavily on families in local communities during these unprecedented times. And, without a significant amount of money saved up, underserved communities have suffered due to layoffs, job loss and COVID-19-related illness and death in the household. Leaving households without any income for weeks.

We promote Financial literacy skills

  1. Build the confidence, knowledge, and skills needed to make financial decisions that promote financial self-sufficiency, stability, and well-being
  2. Financial literacy programs in rural communities opens to use the resources available to them and the opportunities that have not yet been exploited in the local market
  3. Financial literacy empowers consumers, making them knowledgeable about finance in a way that is relevant to their lives and allowing them to use this knowledge to evaluate products and make informed decisions
  4. Those who are financially literate are more likely to save and engage in safe financial products
  5. Our beneficiaries become equipped with a certain level of financial understanding in order to evaluate and compare financial products, such as bank accounts, savings products, credit and loan options

Implementation of Trainings

  1. We assess needs and preferences: Conduct a formal or informal needs assessment to better understand topic areas that are comprehended the least
  2. Identify preferences regarding financial literacy program topics, modes of communication, and frequency of information
  3. Develop culturally relevant, tailored materials : When offering a financial literacy program, especially to those with limited formal education or those for whom English is not their primary language at home, convert complex terms into easy-to-understand language or native dialect, provide culturally relevant materials, offer the materials transcribed in the native dialect, and define/teach terminology
  4. Utilize technology/ digital tools to deliver information: Technology such as mobile devices are used to communicate with the participants about financial literacy, they also use text messages, social media, and other digital tools to deliver content and reminders that can be viewed from anywhere and at any time

We evaluate the impact of the program

  1. Carry out survey that assess the impact of the program on outcomes such as finance-related knowledge and confidence, productivity, and business costs and savings
  2. Use the results of the evaluation to engage in continuous quality improvement of the program

Encourage participants to act on what they've learnt

  1. Choose a model that encourages participants to act on what they have learned to improve their financial decision making.
  2. Financial knowledge is important, but then that knowledge needs to be put to good use.

A complete measure of the effects of a financial literacy program is what the participants go on to do with the information.

We believe that financial literacy encourages financial access and savings which invariably increases economic growth, reduces poverty levels, enhance livelihoods and leads to overall economic development.
